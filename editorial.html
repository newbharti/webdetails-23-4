The Language of Climate Science Has Failed the Public — It Is Time for Plain Speech

Scientists communicate with rigor and caution. Journalists translate with urgency. Neither approach, in isolation, has produced the public understanding that the climate crisis demands. When a climate scientist says "high confidence," the phrase carries precise statistical meaning. When a reader hears it, they often hear "probably." When the same scientist says "very likely," they may be describing a 90 percent probability — a near-certainty by any colloquial standard. The public hears "probably not."

This is not a failure of public intelligence. It is a failure of communication design. The scientific vocabulary of probability was built for peer communication, not public comprehension. For a generation, climate communicators have tried to use it anyway, and for a generation, it has produced a gap between what scientists know and what the public understands that political actors have exploited relentlessly.

The solution is not to abandon scientific precision. It is to establish a second vocabulary — one that accurately represents the same knowledge in terms that are native to the way people actually reason about risk and probability. This is not a small project. It is the most important communication challenge of our time.

The Editors · April 22, 2026 · 6 min read
Analysis · Margaret K. Foster

Geography Is Not Destiny, But It Is Often the Conversation We Refuse to Have

There is a persistent reluctance in policy circles to speak directly about the implications of geography for climate vulnerability. To note that low-lying coastal nations face existential threats that high-altitude continental ones do not; that countries in the tropical band face heat stress that northern latitudes will not experience for decades; that populations built on permafrost or in river deltas or in areas where aquifer depletion is accelerating face futures categorically different from those built on stable, well-watered ground.

This reluctance is partly principled — there is something uncomfortable about telling people that where they were born will shape their survival prospects, because it suggests a determinism that conflicts with commitments to human equality and agency. But the reluctance is also partly convenient. It allows us to discuss climate change as a universal condition affecting everyone equally, when the evidence shows it is a stratified condition affecting the most geographically vulnerable populations first and most severely.

Acknowledging geographic stratification does not mean accepting it as permanent or equitable. It means facing it honestly — which is the prerequisite for addressing it at all.

Margaret K. Foster, Contributing Analyst · April 15, 2026 · 8 min read
Perspective · Dr. James Okoye

We Keep Measuring the Ocean Wrong — and the Consequences Are Showing

The history of oceanographic measurement is a history of underestimating what we did not yet have the tools to see. In the 1970s, we underestimated deep ocean warming because we had no instruments capable of reliably monitoring it. In the 1990s, we underestimated sea level rise because our tide gauge network was too sparse and our satellite altimetry too new. In the 2000s, we underestimated ocean acidification because we were not systematically measuring pH in the depths where many of its most consequential effects were occurring.

Each time, we eventually built better instruments, and each time, what we found was worse than what we had estimated without them. The Argo float network, which has transformed our understanding of deep ocean heat content, was only fully deployed in the mid-2000s. We now have twenty years of comprehensive data — and what it shows is accelerating heat uptake at rates that exceed most of the models built before Argo existed.

The lesson is not that science fails. It is that incomplete measurement produces incomplete understanding, and that the costs of that incompleteness are borne by everyone. We should be investing in measurement capacity as aggressively as we invest in models.

Dr. James Okoye, Oceanography Correspondent · April 8, 2026 · 7 min read
